# Boralverse: Project Context

This document is intended for AI collaborators starting work on the Boralverse project. Read it before asking clarifying questions — most of the basics are here.

---

## What This Project Is

Boralverse is an alternate history and constructed language project. The core creative work has two interlocking threads:

1. **Borlish (Borallesc)** — a fully-developed constructed language descended from Insular Vulgar Latin, with major influences from Old English and Old Norse, spoken on the fictional island of Borland.
2. **The Boralverse world** — an alternate-history Earth in which Borland exists as an inhabited, politically significant island between Great Britain and Denmark (roughly where Doggerland was in deep prehistory).

The two threads are not separable: Borland exists because Borlish exists, and Borlish is motivated by the history of Borland.

---

## The Point of Divergence

The world diverges from real history through the existence of **Borland** (Borlish: *Istr Boral*), a large island in the southern North Sea. In the real world, Doggerland was submerged around 6,500 BCE; in the Boralverse, it persisted — smaller and elevated, but habitable. This single geographic difference cascades into centuries of different settlement patterns, trade routes, politics, and culture across Europe and beyond.

Elsewhere, history tracks the real world closely until specific divergence points multiply — but the world is designed to feel "fractally uncanny": familiar at a glance, subtly different the closer you look.

## Borland: The Core Setting

**Geography**: An island roughly between eastern England and Denmark, positioned in the southern North Sea / Graven Sea. Its heartland is called Angland in the north and Southbar in the south. The capital is **Damvath**, on the River Dam. Major cities include **Axbane** (Roman heritage, oldest city), **Vithor** (west coast, major university), **Noccair** (oldest university), **Cordin**, **Pentrose**, and **Neuvort**.

**History in brief**:
- Pre-Roman: Celtic-speaking inhabitants with Atlantic trade connections
- Roman: Conquered as a province; Latin takes hold
- Post-Roman: Anglo-Saxon and Danish settlers create petty kingdoms; Borlish emerges from Latin spoken among these migrants
- Medieval: Unified Kingdom of Borland through the First Kingdom and Dane-ruled periods; monasteries, universities, guilds
- Late Medieval (1400s–1500s): Wars of Fealty across Albion; Novomundine contact begins; the **Second Great Dying** (1518–1525), a plague that kills roughly 1/7 of Borland's population
- Early Modern: Agricultural revolution, mercantile expansion, abolition of serfdom (legally 1564)
- 17th–18th centuries: Romantic Wars (Vascony vs. Morrack), Long Peace, early industrialization
- 19th century: **Steeplepost** (telegraph/rail equivalent) arrives; Good Game period of alliance-federacies; industrial Workshop period
- 20th century: **Millstone War** (WWI analog); continued development into a modern state

**Government**: Parliamentary monarchy. The parliament lacked a fixed home until the late 18th century; the permanent parliament building in Damvath is the **Palaç Jagovin** (Jacobean Estates).

## Borlish: The Language

### What It Is

Borlish (*Borallesc*) is the only surviving branch of **Insular Romance** — Latin as it evolved on Borland, shaped by the island's unusual history. It is typologically unusual: a Romance language with heavy Germanic substrate and no surviving close relatives.

### Sound System

The 27-letter alphabet includes standard Latin letters plus: **ç** /ts/ or /s/, **ð** /ð/, **æ** /æ/, **œ** /œ/, **ï** /iː/ or /ɪ/.

Key sound changes from Vulgar Latin:
- Intervocalic lenition: /p t k/ > /v ð j/; /b d g/ often deleted
  - e.g. *catēna* > *caðen* "chain"; *lupum* > *lœv* "wolf"
- Loss of most final consonants in polysyllables
- Latin noun declension collapsed entirely; no grammatical gender on nouns; no number marking on nouns
- Affricate /ts/ written **ç** in most positions

Spelling was last standardized in 1870. Orthography is complex:
- `<u>` in certain positions is pronounced /ɪ/ or /iː/ (not /u/)
- Coda `<g>` is pronounced /j/
- Vowel digraphs: `ai ei oi ui` vs `ay ey oy uy` follow systematic rules
- Many silent letters reflecting earlier pronunciation stages

### Grammar Notes

- No grammatical gender
- No number marked on nouns (context and quantifiers carry this)
- Adjectives agree with neither gender nor number (they do inflect for degree)
- Word order is more flexible than English but less so than Latin
- The language has absorbed significant Old English and Old Norse vocabulary, especially in everyday registers

### Sample

Old Borlish (c. 1240) uses Old English–like spelling conventions. Modern Borlish looks like this:

> *Y stal zajadau dy marcað nogtorn accis par lamp fumer eð y lun fragnt de mar...*
> "I walked through the night market lit by oil lamp and the broken light of the sea..."

## Naming Conventions

Borlish names follow Romance patterns modified by the sound changes above. Common features:
- Final vowels often dropped or reduced
- Intervocalic consonants lenited (*Caðoval*, *Borallesc*, *Ydreç*)
- Names may end in consonant clusters uncommon in standard Romance
- Personal names often Latin-origin but phonologically transformed (*Absolon*, *Heudar*, *Loðaç*)

Other nations have their own naming conventions consistent with their linguistic background: Vascon names are Basque/Gascon in feel; British names are Welsh/Celtic; Danish names are North Germanic.

## What the Creator Is Working On

This project is an ongoing solo creative and linguistic construction effort. Work includes:
- Expanding and refining Borlish grammar, vocabulary, and attested texts
- Adding worldbuilding entries for events, people, places, and concepts
- Developing the alternate history timeline with internal consistency
- Producing excerpts (prose, fictional documents, example sentences) that demonstrate the language in use

When assisting, assume familiarity with historical linguistics and alternate history worldbuilding conventions. The creator values internal consistency, plausible historical causality, and linguistic rigor.
