218.5: Taisha

Taisha (Borlish Taisca) is a polity of Gulf Mendeva [NA Gulf], bordered by Hasiny to the east and Mashick to the south. Its coastline stretches from east of the Tocolon [~Brazos River] to southwest of the Nar Mottare [Rio Grande], its capital is Yanaquana [~San Antonio].

Toponymy

The name Taisha comes via Arabic Tayisha' from Carovacian [Caddo] taísha' "welcome", first written down by Morrack cartographers of the New World Company in the early sixteenth century. It is presumed that this word was simply the most common formula the expedition heard on their voyage.

History

The eastern half of modern Taisha was, until the seventeenth century, part of the Hasiny confederacy of Carovacian peoples, from which the neighbouring polity takes its name. The southern and western reaches were under the influence of a series of Mashick [Aztec] vassals, trading upriver along the Tocolon and across the interior.

First Vetomundine contact was with Morrack's New World Company in the 1510s, and through the later sixteenth century Mashick expanded northeast along the coast, establishing frontier towns. Mashick's authority in the far northeast remained thin, and from the middle seventeenth century onward British and Jaughta [Choctaw] ranchers and merchants (operating with the tacit imprimatur of the Kingdom of Britain) moved steadily west into the Tocolon basin. For most of the eighteenth century the region had three overlapping structures of governance: Mashick regents in the south and west, Hasinick towns along the coast, and Carovacian councils in the interior.

The Concordat of Aberdocolon (1850), signed in the namesake port city, formalised this arrangement. The agreement involved the Mashick crown, the newly autonomous Hasinic crown, and elected Carovacian leaders. The Concordat's practical effect was minimal (its commissioners spent most of the subsequent decades arbitrating cattle-rustling disputes) but later Taishan historiography elevated it into a founding document in which the region's three constituent communities were recognised as co-equal parties.

The late nineteenth century saw Mashick reassert central control; the country was reorganised as the northeasternmost province of the Mashick Empire. This period was also the great age of Taishan herdtale [Western] literature, the genre having by then cooled in Hasiny itself. The grounded and unsentimental stories out of Taisha are generally considered to be the pinnacle of the genre.

The Lovac Wars of the 1920s and 30s brought economic collapse, two cycles of conscription and the first bombardment of Yanaquana by airship. A loose self-rule coalition, drawing explicitly on the Concordat's tripartite model, issued the Declaration of Yanaquana in 1936. Mashick, undergoing its own Ubiquitous revolution, did not contest it, granting Taisha legal independence that same year.
