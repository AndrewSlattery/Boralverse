229.5: Who's Who (1800s Edition)

collected notes on various figures active during the nineteenth century.

Poshack Nerwaman (1814–1893) was a Mashick [Mexican] traveller and writer, the most celebrated lady explorer of her generation and a sister of the composer Mollam Bushta. Born in Anawack [Mexico City] to a merchant family, she came early under the spell of Anti-Paradise theology, also called the Deviance movement. She rose to fame during and after the long journeys she made through Cappatia [South America] in the 1840s and '50s: up the great rivers from the Arcabil [Caribbean] coast, over the Anticone Mountains [the Andes], and down to the smoke-coast of Dowahine [Tierra del Fuego]. The accounts she wrote were part natural history, part ethnography of the Cappatian nations, part unrepentant philosophical musings. They stayed in print across the Vetomund [Old World] well into the twentieth century. When she wrote that she preferred 'the company of rivers with four names', some have suggested that she was making an oblique reference to her maid and companion Ignavi.

Wexter Hathwall (1816–1888) was a Merch theorist at Rotcham University [Rochdale]. His magnum opus was the book Astraphor and Light (1859), which gathered the scattered laws of plenty [charge] and barric force which had been described over the previous decades and presented them all as consequences of a single equation—the 'Hathwall relation'. He furthermore showed light itself to be a disturbance in the corresponding aether [field]. The equation predicted the speed of such a disturbance; a decade later, astronomers timing the transits of the jovial phares [Galilean moons] measured the speed of light and found it matched, confirming that light and astraphor were one thing. Hathwall's book drew on correspondence with the spectroscopist Stiláge Anna-Dorota (1788–1863).

Beatreu Marsac (1828–1902) was a Borlish philologist and lexicographer, and she was the guiding hand behind the spelling reform of 1870. A lector at the Noccair Lyceum, Borland's oldest university, she spent the 1860s compiling a dictionary to settle which spellings should win, in tandem with the expansion of mass schooling. Against a vocal and phonetically-minded minority faction, her committee chose to keep various idiosyncratic features of the Borlish orthography [such as coda ‹g› sounded as /j/], while it deprecated etymological polygraphs such as ‹eig› and ‹sç›.
