209.5: Maria Joquinnera

collected notes on a sixteenth-century figure.

Maria Joquinnera (b. c. 1512, d. 1565?), also known as Princess Maria, the Princess of Mendeva and (among other spellings) Dona Quixote, was a woman of Nappe [Lenape] origin, born in the region of the lower Guittoc [Delaware] River, who travelled to the Vetomund [Old World] in the late 1530s and became one of the most remarkable figures of the Intertwining Century. Presented at several Vetomundine courts as a usurped and displaced princess of a Mendevan people, she was by all contemporary accounts a formidable polyglot and political intellect. She is best known for her patronage of the first systematic lexicon of a Nappe [Unami] tongue. The circumstances of her birth, her crossing, and her death remain disputed.

Nomenclature

The name recorded least often, which Maria may have used in private among family and close friends, appears inconsistently in various phonetic renderings and earliest as as Quixote in the account-book (ca. 1538–41) of merchant Bartholomeu fi Acher. The root element has later been tentatively linked to the Nappe word kixuh "sun, moon", which may be connected to the epithet la Dama del Ocàs "the Sunset Lady", attested only once in the Queen of Barcelon's correspondence.

Meanwhile, "Maria" is almost certainly a baptismal name, a common choice in this era and region for converts from non-Christian backgrounds. The name or epithet Joquinnera is a demonyn derivative related to Nappejoquin, the Pronvincial traders' name for the peoples of the lower Guittoc and Esquanna [Susquehanna] rivers. This was borrowed from the Nappe word hoking "land, territory".

Origins and Voyage

Maria's early life is shrouded in mystery. She may indeed have been of saquima [~noble] lineage, but no independent Nappe account survives of a "usurper" (described vaguely in European accounts as one of her father's kinsmen) seizing control of a nation and expelling the family of the former ruler. It cannot be determined how much of the traditional tale of her origins was invented by the Provinical merchants who brought her to Europe, how much was shaped by Maria herself, and what if any actual events underlie it.

Two broad hypotheses to explain Maria's crossing to European between 1539 and 1540 have been advanced. First, she may have emigrated voluntarily as an emissary, out of simple curiosity or in order to flee real danger. This is supported by accounts of her subsequent behaviour, which reflect a clear understanding of her position and a canniness regarding how to best advance it. Alternatively, she may have been abducted or otherwise coerced aboard by the Provincial merchants, as a curiosity for display or a diplomatic prop. This fits the broader unfortunate trend of similar mistreatment of Mendevan peoples more closely, but is harder to reconcile with the deference apparently shown to her as early as her arrival in Marsella [Marseilles], unless she managed to win over the captain (whose identity has unfortunately been lost) and crew in the course of the voyage.

The question of her son, Barthem Joquinnel, further complicates matters. Born within months of Maria's arrival, he could conceivably have been fathered before her voyage or during it. The identity of Barthem's father is not mentioned in surviving contemporary accounts of the birth; Barthem himself, who became a minor diplomat in later life, appears never to have discussed it.

Life in the Vetomund

Maria Joquinnera arrived in a Provence which had been devastated by the Second Great Dying only a couple of decades earlier. She is recorded as having already learnt to speak Doccian [~Occitan] when she arrived in Marsella, and to have picked up several other European tongues (Vask, Burgund, possibly even Borlish) in her first years in Europe.

The fiction (or partial fiction) of a displaced Mendevan princess proved durable, effective and convenient for all parties. It gave the royal courts of Europe a legible category to place her it; it provides the merchants with a compelling story of the western lands to spur further investment; it gave her more room to manoeuvre than any other transplant from Mendeva would receive in this century. She is known to have sojourned at the courts of Burgund, Barcelon and Lombardy; one noblewoman of Furlain described her as "accustomed both to being observed and to observing others without the slightest awe".

Her most enduring legacy is a dictionary of the Nappe tongue (Onomatoir de la Lenga Nappena), in whose front matter she is named as both patron and primary informant. The most complete surviving manuscript contains some 1400 words, along with set phrases and diplomatic formulae, grammatical notes of mediocre accuracy, and a short text which may be a song of mourning or of formal welcome.

The record thins markedly after approximately 1558. It is possible that she attempted to return to Mendeva (she appears alongside attendants on a Provincial vessel's manifest, but may have disembarked before the ocean crossing, as no record of her arrival exists; alternatively, she may have died at sea). Her son visited the Novomund [New World] only once, married a Burgund woman of the lower gentry, and spoke Nappe into adulthood (albeit with a Doccian accent that is recorded as having greatly amused his mother); it is thought that he did not pass the language on to his children.
