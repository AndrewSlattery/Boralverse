219.5: Lumbridge

Lumbridge (Merch /ˈlʊm.bɾɪdʒ/; Saxon Lümbrügh) is a city in the northern plains of Saxony, situated on the Elmenaw river. It is one of the three founding cities of the Stadbund, alongside Hamburgh and Bardwick, and hosts the headquarters of the Shethous, a quasipolitic banking guild.

Toponymy

The earliest attestation of the town's name is in the form Luniburc (tenth century). The first element is usually identified with the placename Hliuni attested two centuries earlier; the second is burg "castle; walled town". The forms Lümbörgh and Lümbrügh (using the component brügh "bridge") competed for some time, but already by the end of the fifteenth century the latter was predominant. This is likely due to the storming and razing of the keep in the thirteenth century and the subsequent completion of the Bridge of Salt landmark (see below), which rendered "castle" an etymological ghost.

History

Founded and fortified with a keep on the Sültberg hill by the tenth century, Lumbridge first grew rich from the export of salt. Toll agreements with Bardwick and Bremen in the 1120s, aiming to restrict levies on salt-barges bound downstream, are among the earliest mercantile contracts between the cities that would later form the Stadbund (also known as the Corvine Concert).

Lumbridge formally renounced the authority of the First German Empire in 1214, seven years after Hamburgh and six after Dorstad. Despite this, the city remained nominally a subject of the Kingdom of Saxony. The religious disruption of the German Secession in subsequent decades had little direct effect on Lumbridge itself, already operating as a mercantile rather than a vassal city. However, the raising of levies to fight against Saxony's in two successive years is what led the city to revolt and destroy the keep in 1251.

By the close of the thirteenth century the Kingdom of Saxony had been reduced to upstream lands in Lepzi and Magdeburgh. Lumbridge became a fully autonomous member of the Stadbund, and by 1326 it had completed the ambitious public work of constructing the Bridge of Salt, a fortified stone span over the Elmenaw which still stands to this day, making it the oldest river-crossing bridge in Europe.

In 1341, Lumbridge's delegation voted in favour of the admission of Wrozlav into the Corvine Concert, unlike its neighbours, having formed an alliance with the Borlish city of Axbane.

Although by the sixteenth century Lumbridge was once again vassal to an empire (the Second German Empire), the city preserved much of its mercantile independence. Emperor Matthias II (r. 1542–1568), attempting to direct Stadbund trade policy, secured only symbolic statements of loyalty from the city council; in reality, the Shethous loans to the imperial treasury gave the merchant nobility of Lumbridge significant leverage. Compare the contemporaneous Drachman merchant dynasty, who dealt similarly with the French, Provincial and Malchassian crowns.

Saxony's ascendancy following the First German War of 1770 brought substantive limits on Stadbund autonomy for the first time since the fourteenth century. Lumbridge retained its seat on the Concert but was obliged to concede customs oversight and a fraction of its salt-monopoly revenues to the Saxon crown at Lepzi.

During the nineteenth-century Workshop period, the Shethous helped to fund Lumbridge's transition from the salt industry to the production more refined alchemical substances.

People
