211.5: Who's Who (1900s Edition)

collected notes on various figures active during the twentieth century.

Mark Deuthex (fl. 1973) was born in Roun [Rouen], the administrative capital of the Drengot Collusion. He spent his early career as an operative for the Red House (the informal name for the Collusion's Ministry for Information); his service there spanned the Collusion's final years and its abrupt collapse in 1961. In civilian life Deuthex took to writing. His 1973 spycraft novel We Wake to Broken Windows drew on his years in the Ministry with a frankness that might have constituted a criminal offence had the Collusion still existed. It remains one of the most widely read firsthand accounts of the Collusion's intelligence apparatus; a sequel was reportedly planned but never completed.

Johan Pamfey (fl. 1971) was a collocker [journalist] for the Borlish Sunday magazine Niacer (a word meaning "pertaining to nests or, metaphorically, to households"), where he contributed most notably a column of political and cultural observation over roughly two decades. He was famous for a reliable willingness to voice unpopular positions, and for various exposés of corporate malfeasance. One of his articles revealed that the Ministry of Health had taken money to downplay the effects of impure imports of detergent chemicals by large companies including Pax and Chandler.

Maldwen Tamaha (b. 1890, fl. 1950) was a long-reigning king of Hasiny [lower watershed of the Mississippi]. He acceded to the throne in 1916 and presided over an unusually steady few decades in Hasiny's history. His reign saw the expansion of the country's sobot [radio] network, and contributed personally in the form of patronage in exchange for weekly speeches broadcast to the nation via the technology. He is also remembered for a personal investment in the expansion of universal primary schooling to the rural parts of the country.
