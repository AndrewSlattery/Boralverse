229.2: souðurr "smoulder"

souðurr /suˈðɪr.r̩/ [sʊˈðɪ.ʀɐ]
- smoulder, to undergo flameless combustion or to burn slowly beneath the surface;
- ferment, compost, to generate internal heat through biological decay or chemical breakdown;
- incubate, fester, to spread silently and multiply before manifesting symptoms;
- oppress, stifle, to trap heat or humidity to an uncomfortable extent;
- to secretly undermine the effectiveness of an organisation or undertaking

Etymology: Middle Borlish derivation of ðurr "kindle, set fire to" with the prefix sou "under". The root descends from Vulgar Latin adūrō "I singe, scorch", itself a prefixed extension of ūrō "I burn", while the prefix descends from Latin preposition sub "under". The sense appearing to have spread from firewood via compost to disease by the eighteenth century, while the weather sense is attested earlier from the sixteenth century. Use in espionage appears in the 1940s as a calque of Welsh marwlosca "I smoulder; I act as a double-agent" in spycraft fiction.
