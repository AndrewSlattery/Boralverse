233.2: aujamat "ghetto"

aujamat /ˌo.ʒaˈmat/ [ˌo.ʝɐˈmat]
- aljama, shtetl, a self-governing minority community especially Jewish or Muslim;
- quarter, ghetto, enclave, a district of an urban area inhabited chiefly by such a community;
- neighbourhood, (in modern urban design) a city subunit built and planned to have most amenities for daily life within walking distance;
- subculture, clique, community, a tight-knit milieu with its own institutions and unwritten rules

Etymology: borrowed in the Middle Borlish period from Vascon aujamat "Muslim quarter or village enjoying some legal autonomy", itself borrowed from Arabic الجَمْع • (al-jamġ) "the gathering, the crowd". The shift from rural to urban senses in northwestern Europe occurred due to the concentration of Jewish and Muslim communities within cities there through the late medieval period.

Metaphorical usage is seen from the seventeeth century, while the modern administrative sense in Borland is due to architect and parliamentarian Penelop Stornel, whose 1911 work laid out a theory of city planning which is still cited to this day.
