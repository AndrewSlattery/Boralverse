226.2: cateu "nasty"

cateu /kaˈtau/ [kɐˈtau]
- wicked, evil, vile, despicable, ill-intentioned or morally reprehensible;
- mean, nasty, spiteful, malicious, disposed to cause (especially petty) harm or taking pleasure in others' misfortune;
- naughty, contrary, badly behaved, wayward, (of a child) given to mischief-making or disobedience;
- foul, grim, miserable, (of weather) unpleasant due to e.g. cold or wet or wind;
- bad, wretched, unfortunate, (of luck or circumstances) not at all conducive to success or flourishing

Etymology: directly from Old Borlish catiu "wretched, pitiable", from Latin captīvus "captive, imprisoned". The semantic shift to immorality (with parallels in e.g. Norman caitiff "cowardly" or Portingale cativo "child" < "puny, scrawny") occurred through the transition to Middle Borlish, motivated by the theological notion of wrongdoers being "captives of sin, or of the devil". The applications to weather and to children are both attested from the early modern period.
