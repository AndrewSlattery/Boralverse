229.4: pedestr "assured"

pedestr /peňąd…õstrŐ©/ [p…™ňąd…õs.t…ź]
- (archaic or historical) on foot, afoot, going or fighting as a footman rather than mounted;
- down-to-earth, grounded, unpretentious, with both feet on the ground;
- clear-sighted, realistic, pragmatic, objective, seeing things as they really are and not blinded by artifice or showmanship;
- at ease, familiar, unfazed, comfortable where another might be daunted;
- sure-footed, assured, self-possessed, acting with the steadiness of one on firm ground

Etymology: Middle Borlish, borrowed from Latin pedester "walking; pertaining to foot-soldiers" to refer to non-mounted combatants in war; this is a derivative of pńďs "foot", whence synonymous pie in Borlish. The image of a person standing on two feet lingers on in the metaphorical extensions even as the literal sense has fallen out of use.
