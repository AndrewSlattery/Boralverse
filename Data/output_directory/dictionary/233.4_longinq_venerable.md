233.4: longinq "venerable"

longinq /lɔnˈʒɪnk/ [lʊnˈʝɪnk]
- long, lengthy, prolonged, lasting, continual, occurring over a great amount of time;
- long-lasting, longtime, venerable, time-honoured, having existing since a distant point in the past (especially as worthy of respect due to this);
- tedious, boring, protracted, lasting so much time that it tries one's patience;
- ancient, antediluvian, belonging to the distant past

Etymology: early modern Borlish, borrowed from Latin longinquus "far-off, remote; prolonged; ancient". This may be a concatenation of the phrase longē hinc "far from here", or may reflect a suffix cognate with Classical German -ungo "-ing". The ambiguity between respectful and derisive use was famously exploited by Borlish aphorist Mary Belvarin at a 1886 soirée in reference to Queen Natalie.
