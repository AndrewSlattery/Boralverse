124.1: Historical Linguistics Exam

excerpt in translation from sample scitation papers for the University of Lester's Faculty of Domain History, published in 1990.

Entrou 5 (trent minut) Argument fair ou ne promoçon ben n'opposson cas dou calscon parmy l'ec thes.
Question 5 (thirty minutes) Argue either in favour of or in opposition to any two of the following theses.

(i) Y parol Anglesc 'silver' a un origin incertan—parol referent all'oç matier son accart des un lengaç vars un autr tras millenner par mejan de sar commarcer lað. L'origin y plausiblessem dell'oc parol (v. sudrabs ne Lithovan, 'asrvae ne Hausan, zirar ne Vasq) com parol trocher es 𒀫𒁍𒌝 (surpum) n'Acadian ataval.
(i) The word 'silver' is of uncertain origin—words referring to the substance have been borrowed between languages for millennia via extensive trade networks. The most likely origin of the word (cf. Lithowan sudrabs, Hausan 'asrvae, Vask zirar) as a trade-word is in ancient Acadian 𒀫𒁍𒌝 (surpum).

(ii) Y benoç de run Sinnesc lonc son, un issar por nom certan e por autr parol d'origin foran des antictað e tesmognað ne polity veçinant (v. lettranç maniocan primal) des y sieçr settem N, caf reconnoscenç ne Cathay sou y regn Kitannesc deut a gimmolt volonter por y polity sevrar deur sell'eç veçin souðer.
(ii) Usage of Sinic runes soundwise, a practice in certain names and other words of foreign origin since antiquity and seen in neighbouring polities (cf. early maniocan [man'yōgana] script) from the seventh century N, gains currency in Cathay during Kitan rule due to purposeful efforts to sunder the polity from their southern neighbours.
