The dialects of Borlish are the only surviving descendants of the Insular variety of Vulgar Latin, which comprised Borlish Latin and British Latin. This variety was not overly divergent from the contemporary (ca. 3rd to 5th century) Latin of the continent, with the following exceptions:
## Insular Latin Innovations

1. Loss of intervocalic /w/, as evidenced in inscriptions:
   - `clam` "key" (continental `clavem`)
   - `denio` "I reach" (continental `devenio`)
2. Loss of word-final /s/, probably preceded by a period of debuccalisation where final /s/ was pronounced /h/. The dating of this sound change is controversial:
   - Fifth-century `a femna` "the women (acc.)" (Classical `hās fēminās` "these women")

The second of these local changes was grammatically significant, as it weakened the distinctions between forms in noun declensions. Indeed, already by the Old Borlish period all number and case distinctions in nouns have been lost.
## Historical Sound Changes

### 1. Posttonic Vowel Loss & Final Syllable Simplification
- V → ∅ / ˈVC\_CV (posttonic vowel loss)
- m → n / \_# if stressed
- m s → ∅ / \_# otherwise

Example: `amictate` /ˌa.mikˈta.te/ "friendship" ← `amīcitātem`
### 2. Palatalisation Part 1
- \[t k\]j \[d g\]j → tʃ dʒ
- k g → tʃ dʒ / \_\[i e\]
- k → j / \_C except \[r l w j\]
### 3. Fortition
- w → β / #\_
- j → dʒ / #\_, V\_V
Example: `iazo` /ˈdʒat.tʃo/ "I lie down" ← `iaceō`
### 4. Consonant Lenition Part 1
- p t k b d g s → b d g β ð ɣ / V\_\[V m l r n j w\]
### 5. Palatalisation Part 2
- nj lj → ɲ ʎ
- Cj → Cʲ → jC except w or word initially
### 6. Final Vowel Reduction
- V → ə / \_# unless stressed
Examples:
- `sabe` /ˈsa.bə/ "he knows" ← `sapit`
- `saibe` /ˈsaj.bə/ "I know" ← `sapiō`
### 7. Weak Consonants Lost
- \[β ð ɣ\] → ∅ / V\_V
### 8. Mid-Vowel Breaking
- ɛ e ɔ o → je e o ow / stressed open
- ɛ ɔ → e o / elsewhere
### 9. Lenition Part 2
- p t k b d g → b d g β ð ɣ / V\_V
- C: → C (degemination)
### 10. Loss of Final Schwa
- ə → ∅ except after consonant clusters
Example: `liei` /ljedʒ/ "halt" ← Insular `laedicum` "collision"
### 11. Affricate Shift
- tʃ dʒ → ts ʒ
### 12. sk Palatalisation & β Shift
- sk β → ʃ v
### 13. Vowel Fronting
- u → y
### 14. Mid-Vowel Rhotic Shift
- e → a / \_rC
- e → i / \_r#
### 15. Full Loss of Schwa
- ə → ∅
### 16. Epenthesis Before r
- ∅ → d / \[n ɲ\]\_r
- ∅ → b / m\_r
Examples:
- `cendre` /ˈtsen.dr̩/ "ash" ← `cinerem`
- `puðir` /pyˈðir/ "rot" ← `pūtēre`
### 17. Vowel Laxing
- i e o y → ɪ ɛ ɔ ʏ / closed syllables
### 18. Final Consonant Devoicing
- b d g v z ð ʒ → p t k f s θ ʃ / \_#
### 19. l-Vocalisation & y-Diphthong Shift
- \[l y\] → w / \_\[C #\]
### 20. Front Vowel Mergers
- \[ɛ ɪ ʏ ɛj\] → ɪ / #\_sC
- \[ɛ ɪ ʏ\] → ɛ / \_w\[C #\]
### 21. Diphthong Shift Part 1
- ai ɛi ɪi → e i əj
- aw ɛw ɔw → o əw u
### 22. Loss of Palatals and ɣ
- ɲ ʎ → n l / #\_
- ɲ ʎ ɣ → jn jl j
Examples:
- `aut` /ot/ "had" ← awt ← ayt ← ayd ← aud ← aˈβu.də ← aˈbu.tə ← aˈbu.to ← Vulgar `habūtus`
- `ismargre` /ɪsˈmar.gr̩/ "arise" ← ɛjsˈmar.grə ← ejsˈmer.grə ← ɛksˈmɛr.gre ← Vulgar `exmergere`
## Late Old Borlish (ca. 1200)

This is the stage, around the turn of the thirteenth century, usually referred to as late Old Borlish:

> …sovravnt il deft nostre saȝntaðesem eð atavalesem n iȝ atrevre golfhavn seȝ hamar dont y verb divin ismetre ac povre paian. peðev soul ez font istovent por vn nov cliȝs d istroienz istablir…

IPA: /soˈvront ɪl dɛwt ˈnostr̩ ˌsajn.taˈðɛsɛm ɛð aˌta.vaˈlɛsɛm nəj aˈtrəwr̩ gɔlfˈhon sej haˈmar dɔnt i vɛrb diˈvɪn ɪsˈme.tr̩ ak ˈpovr̩ paˈʒan ‖ peˈðəw sul ets fɔnt ɪs.toˈvɛnt pɔr yn nu cləjs dɪsˈtrɔjənts is.taˈblɪr/

Translation: …uphold our most sacred and ancient duty to let Gulfhaven be the centre from which we will send the Word of God to pagan lands. We ask only for the necessary funds for a new teachinghouse…
## Middle Borlish Period

### 23. Rhotacisation of Syllabic l
- l → r / C\_# except \[j w\]\_#
This gives Borlish its signature pairs like:
- `capabr` "competent"
- `capablessem` "more competent"
### 24. Aphæresis of Initial isC
- ɪ → ∅ / #\_sC

Like IRL Italian, this means that many words gain an initial vowel only to lose it a millennium later:
- Latin `stannum` "tin" → Old Borlish `istan` "tin" → Modern Borlish `stan` "tin"
However, this process also deletes initial vowels that were there from the start:
- Latin `historia` "story" → Old Borlish `istoir` "story" → Modern Borlish `stoir` "story"
### 25. Break-up of sr Clusters & Hiatus Resolution
- ∅ → t / s\_r
- ∅ → v / \[ɔ o u\]\_V
- uv → ov
The second rule is particularly interesting, since it regenerates some ⟨v⟩ which had been lost in the time of Insular Latin:
- Latin `novellus` "new" → Insular `noellus` "new" → Old Borlish `noel` "new" → Modern Borlish `novel` "new"
### 26. Loss of Front Rounded Vowels
- y ʏ → i ɪ
This provides Borlish with another signature feature, namely ⟨u⟩ /i ɪ/:
- `tu` /ti/ "you (sg)"
- `prun` /prɪn/ "plum"
The loss of front rounded vowels led to some homophonous pairs like `lun lin` /lɪn/ "moon; flax". In many cases this led to one of the pair falling out of use (in this case the latter was replaced with the competing form `linsc`).
### 27. Voicing of Final s
- s → z / \_#
The precise timing of this sound change is hard to determine, since it does not interact with any nearby sound changes. Likely this originated as a sandhi effect before a following vowel-initial word, e.g. `tu es un` /ti ɛ‿zɪn/ "you are a...", before being extended to all contexts.
## Modern Period: Diphthong Shift Part 2 (1600-1800)

### 28. The Great Diphthong Shift
- aj ej ij → ɛj i aj
- əj əw → aj aw
Borlish's answer to the IRL Great Vowel Shift, these changes are somewhat more modest (and take place a little later, between 1600 and 1800). In combination with the loss of front rounded vowels, however, these changes determine much of Borlish's least intuitive orthographic behaviour.
Example: `muy` /maj/ "crumb", where ⟨uy⟩ underwent the changes /yj → ij → aj/.
`Note`: The previous shift reflects the outcomes in the Damvath Standard dialect of Borlish; these recent changes differ greatly in applicability across the island.
## Late Sound Shift (1800s)

### 29. Late Consonant Backing
- ʃ r → x ʀ
The latter of these appears to be part of a general areal phenomenon of backing rhotics. A single word reflecting both these changes is `scuir` /xajʀ/ "boy".
