163.1: This Side of Aldebaran

excerpt in translation from 1987 book This Side of Aldebaran [1], the official print adaptation of the 1986 film of the same name. The story is a touchstone of the contemporary voidtale genre, despite lacking the xenic [alien] peoples so common to the genre.

Jo m'appen contr y pereð ny roum d'occajon sarrað oç se sarvent an entrquïscoir insolemn por machinour vassaler, sgardant tandic Darios faye loit coll'entragl d'un indagour defonct.
I lean against the bulkhead in the cramped utility space that serves as an unofficial break room for junior engineers, watching Darius wrestle with the innards of an antiquated scanner.

Orruð ig l'appragl sey de qualtað militer ma eið doutr par dezein paravant, ant gevrment nuaç ec poussonnant d'un lousc instabr de jom.
The device appears to be military-grade but decades out of date, with exposed circuitry that pulses with an irregular cobalt glow.

Meyon atorn, y locomotour prophthan ny Polletia son un zoum kieð e fix generant oç y paið vibrant sou mell'azig—un son me denuð consolandessem sur ouç tras y semman derran abord.
Around me, the Polletia's superluminal engines create a constant low hum that vibrates through my boots—a sound that's become more comforting than silence over the last weeks on board.

"Yonoscon a tu l'oç ollom trovað?" rou jo, ne my dois a mantien fignt. 
"Where'd you find that relic?" I ask, setting down my maintenance slate.

Darios se ley noc y gas cant i respon la, sy deit çosc stant pernix cos imprefait ne lorry maneyaçon de fil angost com seð.
Darius doesn't look up while he replies, his thick fingers surprisingly nimble as they manipulate silk-thin wires.

[1] originally in Portingale as Atan Aldebaran "Up to Aldebaran".
