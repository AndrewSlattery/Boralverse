63.1: Decline of Empire

excerpt in translation from 1998 work A Baptism of Fire: Christianity and the Graven, written by Agata Naziunas.

…tandic y statu quo de prepondranç imperial sta deja mi dehiscent (ant y dissolution dy Preu Empir Drengoçan all'ovest,
…while the status quo of Imperial hegemony was already falling apart (with the dissolution of the First Drengotian Empire to the west,

l'irrouçon Jennesc ag ci Istr Tyrrhen l'an 1207 eð reyaum ne Magref e ne Libya y dou se declarant autonomy l'an 1237 N/634 هـ), y vray bodanç a mort vene com y Secession German.
the Jeanish invasion of the Tyrrhene Isles in 1207 and the declaration of independent Maghreve and Libyan kingdoms in 1237 N/634 هـ), its true death knell was the German Secession. 

A mesur de dezein porsevent ag Denonçation Paulin casc ducað sevrað (e cas sporadic casc jarlað!) promoveurn ða Rom ben Augsbourg, un panel de fieltað prest a dirompr forgant oc, lon digr, menau colombant vars y sfondranç dell'Empir German com spectacr fablous.
Through the decades after the Pauline Denuncations each individual duchy (and in some cases each county!) declared either for Rome or for Augsburg, in a powderkeg patchwork of allegiances that, of course, led to the collapse of the German Empire in spectacular fashion.

Se poð envou particuler dar ag ducað a Cragn, oc cos diçr gournanç issarr sur y citað vige de Triest, tan com feu uvrauterrem  vars l'est.
Of particular interest is the duchy of Crain, which importantly controlled the prosperous city of Trieste, as well as fertile lands further east.
