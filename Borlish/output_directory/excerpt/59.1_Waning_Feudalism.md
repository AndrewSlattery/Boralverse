59.1: Waning Feudalism

extract in translation from condensed study materials for the course Histoir Concorrent, s16: Mort, Vascel eð y Motion Celest, taught at the Vithor New School.

Interim, valibr es prudenç fair neðan l'eðegl travodaðer starchiscent dy païsanry cas lour y segnour feudal mescomprendr com ða un planfianç abrangent dy ci doujug grader nell'Europ dy siecr seç.
However, one should be careful not to construe the strengthening negotiating position of the peasantry with respect to their landlords as a broad levelling of the hierarchies of sixteenth-century Europe. 

Ne loy plusour apportaurn autortað central (cos usual monarch, ma all'idea remainn y republic latin—particuler Provenç—tan eð y ci patriarchat auster) veloç n'assaut y possenç degredient ag nobiltað vecin por poðir lourell'un meðes condensar.
In many places central authorities (usually monarchs, although bear in mind the Latin republics—in particular Provence—as well as the eastern patriarchates) took swift advantage of the local nobility's declining power in order to consolidate their own. 

Com bustr, argutað es ig ne Rom l'oc proces menau colombant vars y Gran Fragnç e donc vars l'accession dy Triunvirato (may la den ny Capparol 13: Art e Theory Revitalist).
For example, it has been argued that this process in Rome led directly to the Great Fracture and the accession of the Triunvirato (about which more in Section 13: Revitalist Art and Theory).
