UPPER ATMOSPHERE EXPLORATION

===

In the mid-19th century (roughly the 1840s to the 1860s), the maximum altitude a balloon could reach with a recoverable payload was approximately **37,000 feet (about 11.3 kilometers or 7 miles)**.

This altitude limit was not established through theoretical math, but through raw human survival at the very edge of the era's technology.

### The Record-Setting Flight of 1862

The absolute ceiling of mid-19th-century ballooning was demonstrated on September 5, 1862, by British meteorologist James Glaisher and aeronaut Henry Coxwell.

* They ascended in a massive balloon inflated with coal gas, funded by the British Association for the Advancement of Science, to measure atmospheric temperature and humidity.
* Their open-air basket reached an estimated 37,000 feet.
* At this extreme altitude, atmospheric pressure is roughly a quarter of what it is at sea level. Glaisher passed out entirely from hypoxia. Coxwell temporarily lost all sensation in his freezing hands and had to pull the balloon's gas-release valve with his teeth to trigger their descent.
* They managed to land safely, meaning their payload—delicate glass barometers, thermometers, and themselves—was successfully recovered.

### Mid-19th Century Technology Constraints

To understand why they couldn't push higher, it helps to look at the physical materials available to engineers and aeronauts at the time:

* **Envelope Materials:** Balloon envelopes were constructed from heavy natural fibers, primarily tightly woven silk or cotton. To make the fabric airtight, it was painstakingly coated with linseed oil varnishes or dissolved rubber. This resulted in an envelope that was incredibly heavy compared to the synthetic materials used today.
* **Lift Gases:** Hydrogen was the most buoyant gas available, but it was highly flammable and very expensive to generate in large quantities. Coal gas was much cheaper and frequently used, though it provided less lifting power.
* **The Recovery Problem:** Radio telemetry did not exist. The only way to gather atmospheric data was to physically recover the instruments after the flight. While launching an unmanned "sounding balloon" was physically possible, dropping a highly expensive barograph blindly over the countryside and hoping a farmer would find it and return it was too risky. Therefore, a "recoverable payload" almost strictly meant a manned payload, as human operators were needed to navigate the descent and protect the scientific investment.

### The Theoretical Unmanned Ceiling

If a mid-19th-century engineer removed the heavy human crew, the basket, and the survival gear, and built a massive hydrogen balloon strictly to lift a single, heavily padded barometer, it still would not have reached modern stratospheric heights.

The physical limitation was the weight of the balloon itself. As altitude increases and air pressure drops, the hydrogen gas inside the envelope violently expands.

* To prevent the balloon from bursting, the envelope had to be left partially empty at launch or designed with an open bottom (an appendix) to vent the expanding gas.
* As the balloon continually vents gas to survive the ascent, it rapidly loses lift.
* Eventually, the incredibly thin air of the upper atmosphere simply cannot displace enough weight to support the heavy, rubberized silk.

Because of the poor weight-to-lift ratio of 19th-century varnished fabrics, even a perfectly engineered, unmanned hydrogen balloon from 1860 would likely "hit the ceiling" around **45,000 to 50,000 feet (13.7 to 15.2 kilometers)**. It wasn't until the mid-20th century, with the invention of ultra-light, ultra-thin polyethylene plastics, that balloons could finally push past 70,000 feet.

===

The invention of the radiosonde in the late 1920s and early 1930s directly solved the absolute biggest bottlenecks of mid-19th-century ballooning: **payload weight** and the **recovery problem**.

If the 19th-century approach was brute force—hauling men and heavy glass instruments into the freezing sky on massive canvas ships—the radiosonde was the elegant, modern solution that fundamentally changed how we study the atmosphere.

Here is how early radiosondes completely flipped the limitations of the mid-19th century:

### 1. Eliminating the Need for Physical Recovery

As mentioned earlier, the mid-19th century required an aeronaut to physically bring the delicate barometers and thermometers back down to earth to read the data. Even early unmanned "sounding balloons" (invented in the 1890s) carried recording instruments that had to be physically found after they crashed, usually relying on a label offering a cash reward to whatever farmer stumbled across it.

The radiosonde changed the paradigm entirely through **radio telemetry**.

* In 1929 and 1930, inventors like Robert Bureau in France and Pavel Molchanov in the Soviet Union attached small, battery-powered radio transmitters to the meteorological instruments.
* Instead of recording the data on a piece of paper to be read later, the sensors translated temperature and pressure changes into radio signals beamed back to a ground station in real-time.
* **The Result:** The payload was now completely expendable. If the balloon popped and the instrument was lost at sea or destroyed, it didn't matter. The scientists already had the data.

### 2. The Shift in Envelope Materials

By the time the radiosonde was taking off in the 1930s, the materials used for the balloon envelopes had evolved past heavy, varnished silk and cotton.

* Meteorologists began using **extensible rubber** (and later, synthetic neoprene).
* Unlike 19th-century silk, which was rigid and had to be vented to prevent bursting as gas expanded at high altitudes, a rubber balloon stretches.
* A closed rubber weather balloon could be launched partially inflated. As it rose into the low-pressure upper atmosphere, the gas expanded, stretching the rubber wider and wider until the walls became too thin and shattered.

### 3. Shattering the Altitude Ceiling

By combining a lightweight, expendable payload with an expanding rubber envelope, the altitude ceiling skyrocketed.

* **Mid-19C Manned Balloon:** ~37,000 feet (7 miles), carrying hundreds of pounds of men, basket, and canvas.
* **Early Radiosonde (1930s):** ~50,000 to 80,000 feet (10 to 15 miles). Pavel Molchanov's first successful radiosonde flight on January 30, 1930, reached an altitude of roughly 9 kilometers (nearly 30,000 feet) right out of the gate, with later models quickly pushing into the stratosphere.

In short, the radiosonde allowed scientists to trade the massive, heavy, and dangerous manned balloons of the 1860s for cheap, unmanned, disposable bubbles of rubber that could transmit data from the edge of space before popping.

Rubber balloons actually *did* exist during the mid-19th century, but several massive chemical and manufacturing hurdles prevented them from being used for high-altitude atmospheric exploration.

The very first rubber balloon was invented by Michael Faraday in 1824 for use in his laboratory experiments with hydrogen. However, Faraday's balloons were essentially small, crude pouches made by pressing two sheets of tacky rubber together. Transitioning from a small laboratory novelty to a vehicle capable of reaching the stratosphere took another century due to three main problems:

### The Temperature Problem

Before the widespread adoption of vulcanization, natural rubber was highly unstable in varying temperatures.

* At room temperature, it was sticky.
* On hot days, it would melt and sag.
* In the extreme, freezing temperatures of the upper atmosphere, untreated natural rubber would instantly become brittle and shatter like glass.

Charles Goodyear and Thomas Hancock developed the vulcanization process (which uses sulfur and heat to stabilize rubber) around 1839 and 1844. However, refining this process to create highly elastic, thin, and uniformly strong rubber took decades of further industrial development.

### The Manufacturing Bottleneck

To build a high-altitude expandable balloon, the rubber envelope must be incredibly uniform in thickness.

* As a rubber balloon ascends and the gas inside expands, the rubber stretches.
* If there is a single microscopic imperfection, seam, or thin spot in the manufacturing, the balloon will prematurely rupture at that weak point long before it reaches its maximum altitude.
* Mid-19th-century manufacturing simply could not dip or extrude massive, seamless, perfectly uniform rubber spheres. They relied on sewing or gluing panels together, and those seams could not survive the immense stretching required for high-altitude expansion.

### The Weight-to-Payload Paradox

Even if a mid-19th-century engineer could magically manufacture a perfectly uniform, vulcanized rubber balloon, they would have run into a physics problem regarding the payload.

* Expanding rubber weather balloons are strictly reliant on the payload being extremely light.
* Because rubber is much heavier and denser than thin silk or modern plastics, making a rubber balloon large enough to lift heavy 19th-century payloads (like human aeronauts, heavy wooden baskets, and brass-and-glass barometers) would require incredibly thick rubber walls.
* The sheer weight of that thick rubber would immediately cancel out the lifting power of the hydrogen or coal gas inside.

It wasn't until the 1920s and 1930s—when chemical engineering allowed for the mass production of perfectly seamless, ultra-thin latex and neoprene, combined with the invention of featherweight electronic radiosondes—that rubber finally became the ultimate material for high-altitude exploration.

HYDROGEN SPECTRUM

===

The recording of the hydrogen spectrum wasn't a single "eureka" moment, but rather a process that unfolded over several decades as laboratory equipment improved. The credit for first observing and recording it is generally shared among a few key pioneers in the 19th century, depending on whether you mean the first time it was *seen*, the first time it was *identified*, or the first time it was *accurately measured*.

Here is how the discovery unfolded:

### The First Glimpse: Solar Absorption (1814)

The very first time the spectral lines of hydrogen were recorded was by the German physicist **Joseph von Fraunhofer in 1814**, though he didn't know he was looking at hydrogen.

* **How it was done:** Fraunhofer invented a precision spectroscope by passing sunlight through a narrow slit and then a glass prism to separate the light into a rainbow.
* **What he saw:** He noticed hundreds of thin, dark lines interrupting the continuous solar spectrum. He meticulously mapped these dark bands, labeling the most prominent ones with letters. We now know that his "C" line (red) and "F" line (blue-green) were actually the absorption lines of hydrogen gas in the outer atmosphere of the Sun absorbing specific wavelengths of light.

### The First Identification: Electric Discharge (1859)

The first time the *emission* spectrum of hydrogen was successfully isolated in a laboratory and recognized as belonging to a specific gas was by the German physicist and mathematician **Julius Plücker in 1859**.

* **How it was done:** Plücker used a Geissler tube, which is an evacuated glass tube containing a small amount of rarified gas (in this case, hydrogen). He passed a high-voltage electrical current through the tube, causing the gas to glow brightly. He then viewed this glowing light through a prism spectroscope.
* **What he saw:** Instead of a continuous rainbow, Plücker saw three distinct, brightly colored luminous lines on a dark background. He realized that this specific barcode of light was a fundamental characteristic of the hydrogen itself. 



### Precision Measurement: The Ångström Era (1853–1871)

While Plücker isolated the spectrum, it was the Swedish physicist **Anders Jonas Ångström** who is often credited with truly defining it for the scientific community. Beginning his studies in gas spectra in 1853, Ångström eventually recorded the exact wavelengths of hydrogen's visible lines with unprecedented precision.

* **How it was done:** Ångström used similar electrical discharge tubes but analyzed the light using a reflection diffraction grating rather than just a simple prism. This allowed for incredibly accurate measurements of the light's wavelengths.
* **What he recorded:** He definitively measured the four visible spectral lines of hydrogen: one red, one blue-green, and two violet lines. His measurements were so accurate that the Swiss mathematician Johann Balmer later used them in 1885 to crack the mathematical code of the spectrum, creating the famous Balmer formula that predicted exactly where these hydrogen lines would appear.

Ultimately, Fraunhofer saw the shadow of hydrogen first, Plücker isolated its glowing light, and Ångström provided the exact measurements that changed atomic physics forever.

While the logic of your experiment makes perfect sense on paper, it runs into a major barrier involving quantum mechanics and the physical state of the gas. Here is why a simple jar of hydrogen won't produce visible absorption lines:

### The Energy Level Problem

This is the core issue. The visible dark bands that Fraunhofer saw in the solar spectrum correspond to the **Balmer series** of hydrogen. For a hydrogen atom to absorb visible light, its single electron must already be hovering in the *first excited state* (the second energy level).

In a glass container at room temperature, hydrogen's electrons are completely settled into their lowest possible energy state, known as the ground state. Visible light simply doesn't pack the right amount of energy to interact with an electron in the ground state, so the light passes right through unimpeded.

### The Temperature Requirement

To get those electrons bumped up into the first excited state so they are "ready" to absorb visible light, the hydrogen gas needs to be incredibly hot—specifically, the temperature of a star's atmosphere (around 10,000 degrees Celsius). If you tried to heat your glass container to that temperature, the glass would melt and vaporize long before the hydrogen began absorbing visible light.

### Molecular vs. Atomic Gas

Furthermore, the hydrogen in your glass jar would be diatomic—molecules made of two hydrogen atoms bonded together. The distinct spectral lines measured by Ångström belong to single, isolated hydrogen atoms (monatomic hydrogen). To break the molecular bonds and free the individual atoms, you again need either stellar heat or a high-voltage electrical current.

### What *would* the cold hydrogen absorb?

If you have a jar of cold, ground-state hydrogen, it actually *does* absorb light, but it absorbs **ultraviolet (UV) light**, creating what is called the Lyman series of absorption lines. However, your experiment would still fail to see this for two reasons:

* Firelight produces very little ultraviolet radiation compared to a star.
* Standard glass is opaque to most ultraviolet light. The jar itself would block the very light the hydrogen was trying to absorb.

This precise physical hurdle is why the identity of Fraunhofer's solar lines remained a mystery for decades. Scientists couldn't just look at cold gases in jars; it wasn't until they used electrical discharge tubes—which forcefully ripped the hydrogen molecules apart and blasted the electrons into excited states—that they could finally match the laboratory spectrum to the stars.

The ability to discharge electricity through gases actually predates the discovery of the hydrogen spectrum by nearly two centuries, though the early experiments were more like parlor tricks than precision science. The technology evolved from accidental glowing tubes in the 1600s to the highly refined instruments used by 19th-century physicists.

Here is the timeline of how humanity learned to electrify gas:

### The Accidental Discovery: Barometric Light (1675)

The very first observation of electrical discharge in a gas was entirely accidental.

* **The event:** In 1675, the French astronomer Jean Picard was carrying a mercury barometer in the dark. A barometer consists of a glass tube filled with mercury, inverted into a pool of mercury, leaving a vacuum at the top.
* **The phenomenon:** Picard noticed that as the mercury sloshed around, the empty space at the top of the tube glowed with a faint, eerie light.
* **The science:** He didn't know it, but the friction of the mercury rubbing against the glass was generating static electricity. This high voltage was discharging through the low-pressure mercury vapor in the "vacuum" space, causing it to glow.

### The First Intentional Discharge: The Hauksbee Machine (1705)

English scientist Francis Hauksbee was the first to intentionally create a sustained electrical discharge through a gas, turning Picard's accident into a repeatable experiment.

* **The invention:** Hauksbee built a hand-cranked machine that rapidly spun a hollow glass globe. He pumped most of the air out of the globe, leaving a partial vacuum.
* **The discharge:** When he cranked the machine and placed his bare hands against the spinning glass in a dark room, friction built up a massive static charge. The electricity discharged through the rarefied air inside the globe, producing a bright, continuous glow.
* **The limitation:** While it produced light (which Hauksbee called "electric fire"), the gases inside were just random mixtures of air, and the exact pressure and electrical current couldn't be finely controlled.

### The "Electric Egg" Era (Mid-1700s to Early 1800s)

With the invention of the Leyden jar (an early way to store static electricity) and better vacuum pumps, scientists began experimenting with firing sparks through glass vessels of different shapes.

* **The setups:** Researchers built glass chambers, often shaped like eggs or long tubes, and pumped out as much air as possible. They would then release the stored charge from a Leyden jar through metal electrodes at either end.
* **The result:** They produced beautiful, colorful flashes of light that resembled the aurora borealis. However, the discharge was a sudden, violent spark rather than a steady glow, and vacuum pumps still used mechanical leather valves that leaked and couldn't create a pure enough environment to isolate a single gas like hydrogen.

### The Spectroscopic Breakthrough: Geissler and Ruhmkorff (1850s)

To actually study a specific gas's spectrum, scientists needed two things that 18th-century scientists lacked: a continuous, high-voltage electrical current (not just a quick spark) and a near-perfect vacuum so they could inject *only* the gas they wanted to study.

* **The continuous power:** In the 1850s, Heinrich Ruhmkorff perfected the induction coil. It could take a low-voltage battery current and step it up to tens of thousands of volts, delivering a continuous, steady electrical stream.
* **The perfect vacuum:** In 1857, the German glassblower and physicist Heinrich Geissler invented a revolutionary new vacuum pump that used liquid mercury to pull air out of a tube, completely eliminating the leaky mechanical valves of the past.
* **The synthesis:** Geissler created sealed glass tubes with electrodes at both ends, filled them with a tiny, incredibly pure amount of a specific gas, and sealed them permanently. When connected to a Ruhmkorff coil, these "Geissler tubes" produced the bright, steady, pure emission light that Julius Plücker and Anders Jonas Ångström finally used to read the exact barcode of hydrogen.

**The helium question is more nuanced than "discover it earlier"**

Helium in natural gas was already identified in 1903 — when an oil drilling operation in Dexter, Kansas produced a gas geyser that wouldn't burn, and chemists Cady and McFarland found it contained 1.84% helium.
